Clearview to build $55M, 270-unit apartment complex on Metairie mall site: see renderings

  • BY ANTHONY MCAULEY | Staff writer

    Tony McAuley

The owners of the Clearview Mall in Metairie unveiled on Thursday a plan to build a $55 million, 270-unit apartment complex on the site as part of a larger redevelopment of the 35-acre property on Veterans Memorial Boulevard.

The proposed apartment complex at the recently renamed Clearview City Center will be located at the Trenton Street end of the site. It is in addition to a planned $100 million overhaul of the 52-year-old mall complex that will include an Ochsner Health center, outdoor event space, a new Regions bank branch and new retail outlets.

Clearview is partnering with Atlanta-based developer Audubon on the project, which will be called "The Metro at Clearview." Units will range in rent from $1,500 to $3,000 a month and have an average of 925 square feet of space.

Construction is set to begin in May and take two years to complete. The Jefferson Parish Council on Wednesday approved a variance to allow a larger project than had originally been approved.

Site is barely a mile from competitor LCMC Health's East Jefferson General Hospital

The apartment complex will also have two courtyards, including a "resort-style" pool and cabanas, a yoga lawn, lounge area, grilling stations, sitting area and "a Zen garden," the companies announced in a press release Thursday.

Other amenities include a 2,000-square-foot clubroom with multiple seating and entertainment areas, a catering kitchen, a fully furnished fitness center and a sky lounge on the fifth floor that will overlook the redeveloped shopping mall.

The Richards family, which has owned Clearview since its beginnings in 1969, announced the redevelopment of the mall in 2019 and began the first phase of construction in September 2020.

Under that plan, the roof covering the walkway in the mall will be removed and parts of the interior of the current mall will be converted to open-air walkways connecting different parts of the property, with a new "streetscape" added.

Thomas Richards, head of the family corporation that owns the property, said Thursday that the coronavirus pandemic has caused them to rethink the timing and some of the uses that had been in the original plan.

The pandemic has devastated the hotel industry, so plans to build a 150-room hotel at the heart of the property were put off. Likewise, the past year has seen many people working from home as a result of measures to stall the virus' spread, so plans for office space are also being reconsidered.

"The hotel industry in general has taken a really hard hit from COVID," said Richards. "The deal is not dead by any means but the timeframe to develop the hotel is pushed."

He said the owners are considering bringing forward plans for a second phase of apartments that might be located in the floors above the mall's Target outlet, which originally had been earmarked for offices.

Richards also said that the Regions bank branch is nearly finished at the front of the property, with demolition to begin building the Ochsner "super clinic" due to start in March.

Now, with the apartment complex set to start in May, he wants to be able to start demolition on the main mall sight next year rather than hold the project up until the prospects for the hotel industry improve.

The future of the building that currently houses WTIX-FM oldies radio  and a driving school, located on the southwest side of the site, also has yet to be decided, Richards said. That might become the location for the hotel or office space down the road.

The new conception for the Clearview space is part of a wider trend locally and nationally, whereby traditional malls have given way to developments that incorporate living space and entertainment facilities.

The largest anchor tenant at Clearview was for years the Sears department store, which closed in spring 2018 and sold the 14 acres it owned to the Richards family, giving them full control of the 35-acre site.

Sears, like many traditional chain retailers, had been in decline because of the move in the past decade toward online shopping.
